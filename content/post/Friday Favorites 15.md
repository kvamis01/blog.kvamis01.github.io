Happy Friday,

[Cue the music](https://m.youtube.com/watch?v=I3bF0N081TE), brew that pot. I saw my first northern saw-whet owl this week (a pair flew south to neat this spring) and we walked right past it in the tree a few times before noticing it. Makes you wonder about what else you’re not noticing.

Here are 10 interesting things worth sharing this week:

1. “If you ever need a pick-me-up, it’s best to find your way to any gathering where people are hellbent on making things.” [Anna Brones](https://creativefuel.substack.com/p/in-our-hands) on going to a festival of fellow creatives. She’s also enjoying the [joys of using a typewriter](https://creativefuel.substack.com/p/nice-things?triedRedirect=true).
2. It’s Big Summer Book season, and [Jamie Todd Rubin](https://jamierubin.net/2026/05/28/chasing-windmills/) (who reads older biographies and science books) writes about the allure of reading longer books.
3. But maybe instead of a Big Summer Book you’re looking for a [Lil Summer Novel](https://lithub.com/50-great-classic-novels-under-200-pages/)?
4. I first heard [Fred again…](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4iQmPv_dTI0) a few years ago on Tiny Desk. He just finished his USB002 tour, and someone compiled every show into a [108-hour YouTube video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GiXKukOtmeE) that makes for great chill listening.
5. “Before you can make anything, you need a set-up. So when an idea comes, you have the tools to make it happen,” said David Lynch, which [Braxton Haugen shares](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/q2Dv8oI77DQ) in a video about his new set-up. I liked his studio for his [TikTok Tapes series](https://blog.isakkvam.com/post/friday-favorites-12/), which reminds me of my [post about writing spaces](https://blog.isakkvam.com/post/a-writing-space-of-ones-own/) a few weeks ago.
6. Loved [this summary](https://www.tiktok.com/@maralenx/video/7643822791439011094?_r=1&_t=ZT-96iTQDOWqI1) of a [lecture from Paul Schrader](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3NhSZ6RTQgk) (writer of Taxi Driver and Raging Bull) - that great works usually start off as a personal problem, and then become a metaphor. It transforms something deeply personal into something symbolic that a lot of people can relate to.
7. [Hanif](https://www.newyorker.com/culture/essay/our-longing-for-inconvenience) hits on what makes going analog so appealing now: how the discomforts of old technology can make you feel more alive and engaged with the world, but how it’s also a hollow nostalgia that’s increasingly incompatible with our world.
8. And if you’re not going analog, maybe just have more intention about what you use and why. “Every piece of software we use arrives with embedded assumptions about how we should work, think and create,” says [Kai Brach](https://www.densediscovery.com/issues/322).
9. Racket is having the most fun writing online and I’m pretty sure [their article on CRT TVs](https://racketmn.com/crt-tv-minneapolis-mn-video-games-vhs-dvd-marketplace) was made specifically for me (but not really, their readers are read too).
10. I know [Ryan Holiday](https://ryanholiday.net/the-notecard-system-the-key-for-remembering-organizing-and-using-everything-you-read/) uses a notecard system to write his books and vaguely knew others did too, but [Susan Orlean and John McPhee do too](https://aliahabib.substack.com/p/productive-terror-ten-very-different?utm_campaign=posts-open-in-app&triedRedirect=true)—the latter on big 5x9” ones.
