Friday Favorites 19

10 interesting things I'm recommending this week

June 26, 2026

Happy Friday,

Cue the music, brew that pot. I ran my first half marathon last week (it went well!) and am on a summer Stephen King kick (finished “Bag of Bones,” now starting “Joyland”).

Here are 10 interesting things worth sharing this week:

  1. Love these author completionism cards from Rebecca on common threads. There are a few authors I’ve read most of (Annie Dillard, Peter Heller, Robert Caro), but it’d be fun to make these cards for authors I’ve read lots of (King, Vonnegut) and authors I haven’t read yet (Baldwin, Morrison).
  2. It’s tough to give a good speech that’s both interesting and meaningful, but this two-and-a-half minute one from Henry Rollins of Black Flag is one of the best I’ve seen.
  3. Mark Slutsky writes about the feeling of being in good hands, “an intuitive sense that the author knows what they are doing and that the experience will be worth my time.” For me, that’s cracking open Robert Caro, Gabriel Garcia Marquez, and Larry McMurtry.
  4. Despite knowing a few Marvin Gaye tunes, I’d never listened to “What’s Going On” which is #1 on Rolling Stones “500 Greatest Albums of All Time.” It’s very moody, contemplative, and political! Gaye wrote it in one month in 1969 amidst police brutality and Vietnam protests saying “I work best under pressure and when I’m depressed. The world’s never been as depressing as it is right now. We’re killing the planet, killing young men in the streets, and going to war around the world. Human rights … that’s the theme.” Things change and don’t change.
  5. Loving Yumi Sakugawa’s 5-point manifesto “Discipline as Pleasure in Creative Practice,” especially “4. There is pleasure in riding the wave of resistance and alchemizing that energetic block into a sweet, delicioius flow.”
  6. Most articles about reading more boil down to “put away your phone” and “read what you want, not what you think you should.” So I appreciated this essay about returning to reading not from a lack of attention but a lack of curiosity. (It also has that great tip about reading upstream — finding your influences’ influences).
  7. I finished Stephen King’s Bag of Bones, my first Big Book of Summer (which I’m considering is over 600 pages), and the end of the audiobook included a great interview with King that I can’t find online. I love a little nugget about high brow, popular, and lowbrow literature, but that all of it has to have a story — the engine that drives the book forward, because even a Ferrari without an engine — a book of great writing without a story —  can’t get you anywhere.
  8. I used to read a lot of nature writing (Annie Dillard, Thoreau, “The Peregrine,” Edward Abbey) but all of them were a few generations before my time, so I really liked this list of more recent nature writers, many of which I hadn’t heard of.
  9. In praise of goofy writing tips. If it works, it works!
  10. This is way too niche to have mass appeal but the world needs more of that: I’ve been into Tolkien since I read the Hobbit in elementary school. I’ve also liked some metal music, but this year have gotten into loud angry black metal (thanks ICE!). Anyways, this metal concept album from Blind Guardian about The Silmarillion is so great.

Friday Favorites 18

10 interesting things I'm recommending this week

June 19, 2026

Happy Friday,

Cue the music, brew that pot. The orioles are around, a green heron flew through, and a baby skunk and myself scared each other while I was out for a run.

Here are 10 interesting things worth sharing this week:

  1. Love these three roundups of interesting book dedications from Molly Young.
  2. RIP John Basinger, who memorized the entirety of “Paradise Lost” (my copy is 512 pages) by bringing his copy to the gym and memorizing seven new lines on the exercise bike and reviewing the previous 14 lines while lifting weight.
  3. It’s fun but quickly boring to notice the trendy Classics everyone is recommending online — last summer was “Lonesome Dove,” then it was “East of Eden,” and “Count of Monte Cristo” is still popular. So I really liked Tip #4 from Naomi Alderman’s list to google three books you love in quotation marks to find an article talking about those three specifically — which would likely recommend another book you’d like. (Mine? I switched to authors: Annie Dillard, Aldo Leopold, David Gessner. First result worked well!)
  4. Which helped me find this decade-old article about Annie Dillard, one of my favorite writers, and her book “Teaching a Stone to Talk,” one of my favorite books of hers. “That’s what interests Dillard: ‘ultimate concerns’. To address them she relies on two things: her senses and everything she’s read.”
  5. I like older interview-style shows that interview creatives — and I’d never heard of “Bookworm,” a public radio show hosted by Michael Silverblatt from 1989-2023 (!). Here’s a helpful link to the full archive. I’m listening to interviews from the 90s with Elmore Leonard and Tom McGuane.
  6. I’m nearly finished with Stephen King’s “Bag of Bones” about a writer grieving the sudden death of his wife and the ensuing writer’s block. The main character never reaches the top of the best-selling book lists and has to schedule his publications around other, more successful writers — which was inspired by King competing in real life with Tom Clancy.
  7. Really like this list of recommended reading for every summer mood. I’ve paired Stephen King with summer reading since late elementary school, so I’d recommend his for when you want the literary equivalent of walking out of the summer heat into the cool but eerie shade of a quiet forest.
  8. Do today’s writers have regular columns? The media landscape has changed so much, I can’t keep up, but I wonder if a column like Kurt Vonnegut’s or Hunter S. Thompson’s would work today — even if they’re as true today as they were over 25 years ago. When in doubt, write about human ignorance and imperfection, eternal truths.
  9. I feel like I never let myself be bored anymore. (Thanks phones and infinite scroll!) The closest I get is around 4 p.m. when my brain’s so full of information and exhausted from computer work that I do chores without even music or podcasts to fill my thoughts. Here are some things to do while waiting that I probably won’t do but will think about as I unlock my phone.
  10. Finally: end your week with this poem from Mary Oliver.

Friday Favorites 17

10 interesting things I'm recommending this week

June 12, 2026

Happy Friday,

Cue the music, brew that pot. I spent my evenings this week pressure-washing my concrete and listening to Stephen King narrate his book “Bag of Bones.” Recommend!

Here are 10 interesting things worth sharing this week:

  1. Really enjoying Austin Kleon’s new book “Don’t Call It Art” and was delighted to find a short sub-chapter about having fun — which pairs with a question I’ve been asking myself this spring: What if this were fun?
  2. I’m always fascinated by how writers spent their days before the internet (both the constant procrastination and the endless barrage of information), so this article with Louis L’Amour’s typewritten weekly to-do lists (nevermind the title) was so fun to read. It feels simultaneously so similar and yet dissimilar to modern routines.
  3. Isn’t it fun to learn a new word for something you already do? I decided to blog more often this year about what I’m reading, thinking, and doing, and turns out that’s a “digital garden” of exploring and connecting ideas.
  4. There’s a plethora of “the internet has made it harder to read” articles, but I found this article interesting because it wondered if having a job where you read bad writing (emails, reports, social media, etc.) all day makes it harder to wind down with a good book.
  5. Meanwhile, here’s a guy who reads good writing every day for his job: “Not every executive can read every book,” he explains. “Everybody’s busy and they don’t have time to read everything.” Clark Speicher works for Hollywood reads a book every day or two (especially Lit), surgically breaks it down beat by beat, and recommends how it be adapted.
  6. Every since I started percussion in band in fifth grade, I’ve been tapping on surfaces around me. So I decided to level up and learn the sticking for a tough song: the drum intro for Van Halen’s Hot for Teacher. There’s something satisfying about working through how a song works, it feels similar to doing a Rubik’s Cube in my opinion.
  7. There is SO much energy in this Die Spitz show on KEXP, this is a blast.
  8. Fela Kuti was a big inspiration for David Byrne and Brian Eno for Talking Heads’ “Remain in Light” album, but I didn’t know Hanif Abduraqib was a creative producer for the recent podcast mini-series “Fela Kuti: Fear No Man.” Plus Jad Abumrad from Radiolab!
  9. Love this Instagram account from Nicholas Triolo sharing 200 books about ecology and justice in 200 words or less.
  10. With summer heating up, now’s a perfect time to watch an old movie that pairs with higher temps.

What if this were fun?

June 9, 2026

In the 2010s, I heard Tim Ferriss share a phrase that guided his work: what would this look like if this were easy?

A question I’ve been asking myself this spring is: What if this were fun?

Most of my best work has come from doing something fun. It’s the rule of cool! Having fun is fun, and fun is contagious. You can tell when a writer is bored by their subject and putting no effort into making it more engaging or readable. You can also tell when a writer is having fun, like Bill Bryson or Hunter S. Thompson or Charles Portis.

Friday Favorites 16

10 interesting things I'm recommending this week

June 5, 2026

Happy Friday,

Cue the music, brew that pot. It’s officially summer, and I’m soaking up LONG and HUMID summer days here in Minnesota and making plans for the summer solstice.

Here are 10 interesting things worth sharing this week:

  1. A new PBS documentary about the Mississippi River looks SO good, can’t wait for this to come out.
  2. I love following Dave Fogler’s YouTube channel for his time-intensive, pointless house modifications — but also for the midcentury vibes and fun vlogging, and his latest on eliminating corporate logos on his truck is a fun watch.
  3. “I have faith that these typewriters are going to lead me somewhere. I don’t know where, but I hope somewhere interesting.” In which Ruth Ozeki discovers the joys of a typewriter. I also enjoyed learning about her writing process journal to aid her writing process.
  4. If you’re having trouble writing, try reading, watching movies, going to a museum, or going out with a friend. Austin Kleon’s book “Don’t Call It Art” came out this week, and Chapter #7 is “Problems of output are problems of input.” (He’s making the book tour rounds, and I was happy to see him on the “A Reading Life” substack, too.)
  5. I’ve been carrying a Field Notes journal in my back pocket since 2019, and I immediately ordered both sets of their new “Explore America” series, which reminds me of their fantastic “National Parks” series.
  6. Larry McMurtry would write five pages a day on his Hermes 3000 typewriter, even stopping in the middle of a sentence to avoid going over his daily limit. I loved reading the Tracy Daugherty biography on him last summer, and the new “Western Star” bio is on my desk now.
  7. My Big Summer Book pick right now is “Bag of Bones” (my version is 736 but it’s a small mass market paperback), and I am loving the eerie, summery tension laced throughout this one. I’m only 20% in, but I’m curious why this hasn’t been held up as one of his better books?
  8. Resonated this TikTok about how creative backlogs can be a block. I’ve had a bunch of creative ideas rattling around in my head for too long, and doing them and moving on sounds easier and more fun than thinking “I really should do that soon.” Should!
  9. There’s a plethora of book clubs and read-alongs on Substack that I’m finally going to give one a try and join Many Meetings on a read-through of The Silmarillion — a book I’ve read a few times but never feel like I quite grasp! Third time’s a charm?
  10. “Doing the thing every day is easier than not doing the thing every day.” I regret that rigid consistency helps me do the things I want to do but I do wish I was less all-or-nothing about it.
